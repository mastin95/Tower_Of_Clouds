# Characters & Archetypes Framework

## Archetypes vs. Stereotypes

An **archetype** is a recurring pattern of human experience given form.
A **stereotype** is a pattern that stopped evolving.

The difference: archetypes have *a wound* that makes the pattern feel specific and alive. Stereotypes don't.

Before creating any character or archetype, answer:
- What does this character want more than anything?
- What do they believe about the world that is *wrong*?
- What wound shaped them — and do they know it?
- What would they sacrifice, and what would they never sacrifice?

---

## The Archetypal Roles Framework

For worldbuilding (not story structure), think in **social roles** that emerge from the world's specific tensions:

### Role Definition Template
```
**Archetype Name:** [Evocative, specific — not "The Warrior"]
**Social Function:** [What role do they fill in this world's power structure?]
**Core Belief:** [What do they think the world is, fundamentally?]
**The Lie:** [What false belief sustains them?]
**The Truth They Avoid:** [What would break them if they faced it?]
**Desire:** [Surface want]
**Need:** [Deeper, often unconscious]
**Wound:** [What shaped this pattern in this world?]
**Relationship to Power:** [Do they serve it, resist it, embody it, or flee it?]
**Thematic Function:** [What idea does this archetype embody or challenge?]
```

## Archetypal Pairs

The most generative archetypes come in **pairs that are mirrors**, not opposites:

- They share a wound but respond to it differently
- They want the same thing but through incompatible means
- One is what the other could have become

Examples of mirror pairs:
- The prophet who speaks for a god / The apostate who heard the same god and said no
- The guardian who protects through control / The liberator who protects through sacrifice
- The true believer / The pragmatist who uses belief as a tool

Design archetypes in pairs whenever possible. The tension between them is the interesting space.

---

## Archetypes as World-Mirrors

In worldbuilding, archetypes should **make the world's values visible**. Ask:

- What kind of person does this world **reward**? (That person is a window into the world's values)
- What kind of person does this world **punish or exile**? (That person is the world's shadow)
- What kind of person does this world **create in abundance** — not by design, but as a side effect?

The last question is often the most revealing.

---

## NPC Depth Heuristic

For significant worldbuilding NPCs (not every background extra), they need:

1. **One thing they do that surprises you** — something that doesn't fit the archetype cleanly
2. **One relationship that complicates their worldview** — someone who makes them doubt
3. **One secret** — not for dramatic reveal, but because it shapes every interaction they have

Without these three, they're furniture, not characters.

---

## Thematic Resonance Check

- Does this archetype embody a genuine human tension, or just a genre convention?
- Does their wound connect to the world's central themes?
- Can a player/reader have a complex reaction to this character — not just sympathy or antipathy, but *understanding*?
