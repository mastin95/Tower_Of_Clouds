# Magic Systems & Artifacts Framework

## Sanderson's Laws (Applied)

### First Law
*"An author's ability to solve conflict with magic is directly proportional to how well the reader understands said magic."*

**Applied rule**: If magic solves a problem, the reader must have understood the rules beforehand. Surprise powers feel like cheating. Foreshadowed powers feel like payoff. 

For games: if a card or mechanic uses the magic system, the player must be able to *predict* its behavior from established rules.

### Second Law — **Start here**
*"Limitations are more interesting than powers."*

**Applied rule**: Design the cost, restriction, and failure mode *before* designing what the magic does. The limitation is the interesting design space.

Questions to answer first:
- What does using this magic **cost**? (Not just resources — what does it take from you?)
- What can this magic **never** do, no matter what?
- What happens when it **fails** or is **pushed too far**?
- Who **cannot** use this magic, and why?

### Third Law
*"Expand what you have before adding something new."*

**Applied rule**: Before adding a new magical element, ask whether an existing element can be deepened to fill the same role. Magic systems become incoherent when they accumulate exceptions faster than rules.

## Magic System Architecture

### The Source
Where does magical power come from?

- **Internal** (from the self): Costs the user something personal — vitality, memory, sanity, time
- **External** (from the world): Costs access, ritual, or relationship — you must earn or borrow power
- **Transactional** (from a third party): A deity, spirit, or entity grants power — with strings attached

The source should resonate with the world's themes. A world about faith should have magic that demands something that *feels like faith*.

### Magic System Template
```
**Name:**
**Source:** [Where does power come from?]
**Cost:** [What does using it take — from the user, the world, or a third party?]
**Hard Limitation:** [What can it NEVER do?]
**Soft Limitation:** [What is it bad at / inefficient at?]
**Failure Mode:** [What happens when it's misused, overused, or fails?]
**Who Can Use It:** [What determines access?]
**Who Cannot:** [Equally important — who is excluded and why?]
**Social Status:** [How does society regard practitioners? Fear? Reverence? Contempt?]
**Thematic Echo:** [What does this magic *mean* in the context of the world's themes?]
**Game Mechanic Hook:** [Optional — how does this echo into card/mechanic design?]
```

## Artifact Design

Artifacts are not just magic items — they are **arguments about the world**.

A great artifact:
1. **Has a history** that tells you something about the world
2. **Has a cost** that makes you think twice about using it
3. **Has a perspective** — it was made by someone with beliefs, and those beliefs are embedded in it
4. **Creates a dilemma** — using it compromises something

### Artifact Template
```
**Name:**
**What it does:** [Mechanical effect — be precise]
**What it costs:** [To use / to carry / to own]
**Who made it and why:** [The intent of its creation]
**What it wants:** [Optional — if the artifact has will or memory]
**The compromise:** [What do you give up or become by using this?]
**Lore fragment:** [1-3 sentences of in-world text — a journal entry, inscription, or testimony]
**Game mechanic hook:** [Optional — how does this artifact translate to gameplay?]
```

## The "So What?" Test for Magic

After designing any magical element, ask:
- **Narratively**: What story becomes possible because this magic exists?
- **Thematically**: What does this magic *mean*? What does it say about the world?
- **Mechanically**: (For games) Does this create interesting choices, or just power?

If the answer to "thematically" is "nothing in particular", the magic is decoration. Redesign the cost or source until it resonates.

---

## Thematic Resonance Check

- Does the cost of magic reflect what the world values or fears?
- Does the limitation create genuine dramatic tension — not just balance?
- Does an artifact tell you something true about the world's history and wounds?
