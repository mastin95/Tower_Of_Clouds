# Pantheon & Religion Framework

## Core Design Questions

Before designing any deity or religion, answer:

1. **What does this god WANT?** (Gods with desires are more interesting than gods with portfolios)
2. **What can this god NOT do?** (Limitations define character more than powers)
3. **What does worshipping this god COST?** (Faith without cost is decoration)
4. **What does this god's existence EXPLAIN?** (Why does this god need to exist in this world?)
5. **Who is this god's enemy?** (Conflict is the engine of mythology)

---

## The Pantheon Structure

### Avoid the Portfolio Trap
Don't assign gods domains like job titles ("god of fire", "god of war"). Instead, assign them **perspectives** or **philosophies**:

- Weak: "God of the Sea"
- Strong: "A god who believes all things return to formlessness — and is not sad about it"

### Pantheon Dynamics
A pantheon is a family, a court, or a faction system — not a filing cabinet. Ask:

- Which gods have unresolved conflict with each other?
- Which alliance is secretly fragile?
- Which god was exiled, diminished, or forgotten — and why?
- Which god wants something the others won't allow?

### The Three Tiers (optional structure)
- **Primordials / Makers**: Pre-moral forces that created reality. Not worshipped so much as feared or negotiated with.
- **True Gods**: Entities with will, ego, and investment in mortal affairs. These are the ones with churches.
- **Demigods / Saints / Avatars**: Mortal-adjacent beings elevated or broken by divine contact.

---

## Religion as System

Religion in a world is not just belief — it's a **social technology**. Ask:

- What does the religion **do** for its followers? (Comfort? Power? Community? Justification?)
- What does the religion **demand**? (Behavior, sacrifice, hierarchy, belief?)
- Who **controls** the religion — and what do they gain?
- What is the religion's **relationship with the state**? (Opposed, aligned, identical?)
- What is considered **heresy** — and what happens to heretics?

## Writing Divine Entities

When describing a god, structure the entry as:

```
### [God's Name]
**Core Philosophy:** [One sentence — what does this entity fundamentally believe?]
**Domain:** [Not a job title — a perspective or force]
**Desire:** [What does this god want, always, obsessively?]
**Limitation / Wound:** [What can it not do? What is it afraid of? What broke it?]
**Cost of Worship:** [What does genuine faith in this god demand of mortals?]
**Mythology Hook:** [One story that defines how mortals understand this god]
**Relation to Pantheon:** [Ally of X because __, enemy of Y because __]
**Mechanical Echo:** [Optional — how does this god's nature echo in game mechanics?]
```

---

## Thematic Resonance Check

After designing any divine element, ask:
- Does this god's existence make the world's central themes *feel* more real?
- Does worshipping this god have a cost that produces interesting dramatic situations?
- Could a player/reader have a *genuine opinion* about this god — admire, fear, or despise them?

If all three are "no", the god needs more character.
